Museum of failures
What didn't work,
and what I learned.
Most engineering portfolios exclusively demonstrate wins. This page serves as a transparent look at everything else—communities that collapsed, projects that stalled, and systems that were architecturally correct but failed conceptually. In software development, failures teach you mechanics.
The Greatest Failure
ARO1 — The Abandoned Rolls Royce
A fully funded physical venture killed by external manipulation
physical business
TriedBuilt a highly-performant React funnel for a physical automobile detailing company. Invested ~500€ of my own capital into professional gear, secured a domain, and mapped a fully legally compliant Rinseless-wash strategy.
Why it failedI presented the fully-armed business to a partner who instantly convinced me it wouldn't scale because the market was "too saturated." I allowed his doubt to manipulate my vision, pivoting to a CAD project that ultimately died.
LearnedWorking alone provides undeniable clarity. If you build it, trust your own momentum. I left a perfectly functional Rolls Royce abandoned under a tarp just because someone told me it wouldn't drive. Never abandon a proven idea for someone else's doubt.
The one that still haunts me
ARO1 — The Rolls Royce Left in a Garage
Mobile car detailing business — fully armed, never deployed
venture
TriedBuilt a complete physical business from scratch — a React landing page on a paid domain, €400-500 of professional-grade detailing equipment ordered and sitting in boxes, and a legal operational strategy built around rinseless washing to bypass environmental restrictions. The plan was to drive to shopping centre parking lots and offer detailing on the spot. Everything was ready.
Why it failedShowed the full ecosystem to an incoming partner. They decided instantly it wouldn't go far — too many competitors, physical labour is a dead end, pivot to digital. I listened. The equipment stayed in boxes. The domain expired. We pivoted to a CAD project that died in the ideation phase without a single real action ever being taken.
LearnedI let an external voice kill something that was fully built and ready to move. The business didn't fail — it was never given the chance to. The Rolls Royce wasn't broken. It just hasn't been driven yet.
Discord Communities & Hubs
G:ZKDU — Global:Zonal Community Development
A conceptual macro-hub for self-development and networking
community
TriedBuild a vibrant hub where people could collaborate exclusively on innovative side-projects while hosting a casual fun-zone interface.
Why it failedDevelopment paralysis. I attempted to curate the absolute perfect product structure internally before deploying it externally. I built the structure, but never shipped it.
LearnedSeeking a perfect launch permanently prevents a real one. Minimum viable product first, refine later.
Beehoove
A complex gamified 'Learn-to-Earn' social experiment
community
TriedProgrammed a massive multi-category network spanning fitness to software engineering. Users engaged and learned to generate digital points, which mapped directly to real PayPal bounties.
Why it failedInformation overload. The volume of categories was paralyzing, and it proved impossible to establish an initial critical mass of people.
LearnedComplexity kills initial network adoption. You must establish a rigid core loop before scaling out supplementary features.
Hangoutspot
A completely generalized social relaxation server
community
TriedCurated a simple, low-pressure application space purely for individuals to network and communicate with zero underlying agenda.
Why it failedAn absolute lack of defined mechanical purpose. "A place to relax" is simply not a strong enough utility vector to drive acquisition.
LearnedVibes do not drive platforms. People adopt software because it solves a problem; they only stay for the feeling.
E-Commerce & Digital Markets
WMShop
A Discord storefront explicitly backed by GPU-mining yields
e-commerce
TriedLeveraged GPU cryptocurrency mining logic ("Salad") to organically fund the purchase of Steam Keys. Integrated PayPal APIs and created a physical Discord storefront.
Why it failedThe backend financial loop and application pipeline worked flawlessly. It failed strictly due to an inability to market an unverified Discord storefront to internet traffic.
LearnedA perfect backend with zero distribution is a dead asset. Engineers often fail to recognize that building the code is the easiest phase.
PlayPortal
The secondary structural evolution of WMShop
e-commerce
TriedCompletely rebuilt the game shop pipeline, abandoning the Discord text-storefront for a dedicated sellpass.io backend to force legitimate checkout flows.
Why it failedHit the exact same wall as WMShop. An incredibly clean product, but zero ability to reach an audience. Solved the wrong problem a second time.
LearnedRebuilding an infrastructure does not fix a marketing bottleneck. Engineering comfort cannot fix sales gaps.
Statboost Gaming Services
Grey-market arbitrage and manual grinding firm
e-commerce
TriedLocated obscure, cheap keys and resold them below active market value while simultaneously attempting to provide manual resource grinding protocols in Rust and Sea of Thieves.
Why it failedFailure to gain traction. Attempted to compete in an incredibly crowded space with absolutely zero differentiation logic or network backing.
Learned"Being cheaper" is an unsustainable business model if the market doesn't know your business exists to begin with.
Full-Stack Frameworks
Roswaldi Web Agency
High-performance React corporate frontends
development
TriedEngineered a multi-tier web agency leveraging React to target established businesses. Successfully built and deployed premium portfolio demonstration logic under the Roswaldi namespace.
Why it failedThe underlying product aesthetic and framework was elite. The project was simply killed by circumstance, as the co-founders schedules permanently detached.
LearnedA scalable deployment stack means absolutely nothing without explicit time commitment. Co-founder alignment is a structural necessity.
There is no prestige in hiding massive architectural or business failures. The projects in this list actively shaped how I structure my code configurations today.
